Reducible vs Irreducible Errors

Suppose we have just fit a model of the form \[ Y = f(X) + \varepsilon \] to data \((x_i,y_i)\) and are at the stage where we have validated that the resulting predictions \[ \hat{y}_i = \hat{f}(x_i) \] are close to the \(y_i\) (for example, by looking at \(R^2\)), but the model isnt perfect and we think we could do better. Time to look at the individual errors made by our model. Since \[ y_i - \hat{y} = f(x_i) + \varepsilon_i - \hat{f}(x_i) = f(x_i) - \hat{f}(x_i)+ \varepsilon_i \] where \(\varepsilon_i\) is the contribution of the noise to the \(i\)th data point, there are thus two sources of errors to consider

  1. Reducible error: Error introduced by the difference between \(f\) and \(\hat{f}\) (approximation error)
  2. Irreducible error: Error resulting from the noise

There is little one can do about irreducible error, other than estimating its variance and incorporating this uncertainty into confidence intervals and prediction intervals. Reducible error, however, can be minimized through the following approaches:

  1. Optimizing Model Parameters:
    Use maths to find the best-fitting parameters for a chosen model. For example, in a simple linear model (SLM), this involves minimizing the residual sum of squares to estimate \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\) using least squares.

  2. Selecting an Appropriate Model Form:
    Choose a functional form for \(f(x)\) that closely matches the underlying relationship in the data. For instance, we only want to use a linear model if the trend is linear.

  3. Ensuring Data Quality:
    Minimize errors in the data itself by addressing issues such as measurement error, outliers, or missing values.

Point 1 isnt an issue for SLM since we know how to find the best slope and intercept if the relationships actually is linear, but in these notes well look at some things you have to be careful of in 2 and 3.

Identifying Nonlinearity

Residuals are defined as the individual errors \[ e_i = y_i- \hat{y}_i \] They represent whats left over after accounting for the trend you think you have identified. Most techniques for identifying issues with SLM or other regression models (a broad topic referred to as regression diagnostics) involve looking at the distribution of residuals to identify problems.

If the relationship between \(y\) and \(x\) truly is linear and we plot the residuals \(e_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i\) against \(x_i\) or the predicted values \(\hat{y}_i\), then there should be no systematic pattern in how the residuals depend on \(x\). If there is, it may be evidence of a nonlinear relationship.

To see why, suppose that I generate some fake data according to a quadratic model

set.seed(123)
x=seq(-10,10,by=0.1)
y=2*x^2-3*x+5+rnorm(length(x))
df=data.frame(x=x,y=y)
df %>% ggplot(aes(x=x,y=y))+geom_point()+
  geom_smooth(method="lm",se=FALSE)
## `geom_smooth()` using formula = 'y ~ x'

Notice how there is a pattern to where we over and under estimate \(y\): when \(\hat{y}\) is small or large, we underestimate and when \(\hat{y}\) is in the middle, we overestimate. If calculate and plot the residuals vs fitted values, we will see this pattern show up as a giant U:

fake_lm=lm(y~x,data=df)
df %>% 
  mutate(yhat=predict(fake_lm,df),res=y-yhat) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x=yhat,y=res))+geom_point()+
  labs(x="Predicted Values",y="Residuals")

In contrast, if the data actually is linear, well see a random scattering of errors

set.seed(123)
x=seq(-10,10,by=0.1)

Outliers

The third issue mentioned earlier is quality of data. While you do not often have control over the data you receive as statisticians (unless you are fortunate enough to be involved in the data collection process), you can still identify unusual points or points that have a larger than usual influence on the SLM fit. The former are called outliers and the latter, high leverage points.

Both outliers and high-leverage points can have dramatic effects on your model accuracy, but we are only going to talk about the former here because I am getting tired. There are not hard and fast rules for identifying or dealing with outliers, but they are always worth considering and Ill give you some advice below.

Outliers are points with unusually high residuals. They are very punk rock and dont conform to the standards. Sometimes you can pick them out immediately by plotting the data, but another common method is to look at the distribution of standardized (sometimes called studentized) residuals.

Standardized residuals are the regular residuals converted to z-scores. Assuming we have already ruled out bias due to nonlinearity, thee residuals should have mean 0 so we can covert to z-scores if we divide by their standard deviation. The common method for estimated the standard deviation of residuals is called the Residual Standard Error (RSE): \[ RSE = \sqrt{SSE/(n-2)} \] Its basically the RMSE, but we divide by \(n-2\) because of the degrees of freedom2

Standardized residuals are easier to use for outlier identification because they do not depend on units of measurement so one can say that any values below say 2 (in absolute value) are unusual. Lets look at them for the TV model as an example

n=nrow(Advertising)
rse=sqrt(sum((Advertising$log_res)^2)/(n-2))
Advertising=Advertising %>% 
  mutate(log_stres=log_res/rse)
ggplot(Advertising,aes(x=yhat_TV,y=log_stres))+
  geom_point()

ggplot(Advertising,aes(x=log_stres))+geom_histogram(binwidth=0.5,color="black",fill="steelblue")

These two plots reveal that (1) the distribution of standardized residuals is pretty symmetric and reasonably approximated by a standard normal distribution and (2) there may be a couple of outliers in the lower end of the distribution (points with standardized residuals close to -2). Lets look at those observations in the lowest bin:

We have two choices about what to do with these outliers:

  1. Take them out and refit: best choice if the company identifies these as experimental situations that will not be reproduced in the future
  2. Leave them in and keep model as is: best choice if the company identifies them as typical strategies.

In this case, excluding the two points does lead to a slight increase in R^2:
